Collections


Collection Composition
Our game collection is divided into two main categories; console and tabletop. Console will consist of any game with which a video game console is needed to play, such as a PlayStation, Xbox, or Nintendo Wii while the tabletop game collection consists of dice, card, and board games. Console games will be available for one week checkout while the tabletop games will only be available for in library use. Both types of materials will be held at the main desk and can be checked out using a library card. The library will retain one copy of available console titles, one copy of each board game, and two copies of each dice/card based game.

Collection Accessibility
All dice, card and board game titles will focus on games that can be completed in under an hour in order to maintain a good play experience for the patrons while giving the players enough time to solidly complete multiple games. The library website will have multiple links to YouTube video tutorial of the tabletop games available, as well as embedded links to game trailers of console games available at the library. This works to show the patrons the materials available and allows them to have a better understanding of the material they are checking out. The board games for example often have a multitude of rules that become intuitive after the first play through, but the accessibility of a video may make it more appealing to patrons who do not want to comb over a difficult rulebook. Furthermore the library will have a scanned PDF of the tabletop games’ rules available to the patrons.

Organization


Metadata
To begin, the data about these items will be stored in a database that’s been built to take advantage of FRBR. Because some of these items can have different interpretations, it makes sense to organize the data in this way.

The collection is comprised of two categories of games. Video games, software that is played on specialized devices, and board games, games that are played with physical pieces, usually with a playing board and pieces.

The two do share some metadata, description, age rating, release date, and number of players. For video games only, we’d want to track how many discs the game had, and what hardware it is compatible with. And for board games, we’d want to know how many pieces are used, what the average play time is, and how much physical size the game takes up.

Physical Organization
The physical collection would have the video games shelved in a similar manner to movies. There would be no genre subdivision, games would simply be organized by type of system they’re playable on and then by title. Disc cases would be prominently displayed with their covers facing outwards, and would be empty. In order to obtain the actual disc, the case would be brought to the front desk and be filled with the appropriate contents from a staff only shelf.

The board games would be stacked like books, with the title on their boxes facing outward. There would be no subdivision, the games would simply be organized in alphabetical order. The boxes would be empty, like the video games, and the required components would be handed out by the staff at the checkout desk.

The reason for having empty boxes for both video games and board games is to prevent theft and the potential loss of contents, as both are expensive to replace.

Subject Access

The subject vocabulary would be tightly controlled. Since the materials aren’t very diverse, it would be easy to have a subject authority that was fairly consistent.

Preservation

To ensure that the material is well maintained the library will clean discs upon their return. Given that many of the tabletop pieces are durable plastic they do not need much upkeep, cards however can be placed into card sleeves to extend their usability relatively cheaply. In extreme cases lamination can be used to extend the life of the more fragile game components.
